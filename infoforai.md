Website Research Brief: Home Salon (Women / Men Split)
1) Goals and success metrics

Business goals: Establish a professional online presence for the home-based salon, showcase the stylist’s work to build trust, and convert visitors into appointments. The site should help fill the schedule by appealing to both women and men, increasing overall client volume and retention. It must also reinforce the salon’s brand (turquoise/teal theme) and local reputation.

Visitor goals: Quickly find services relevant to their needs (women’s styling or men’s grooming), see proof of quality (photos, reviews), and easily book an appointment or contact the stylist. Visitors want a smooth, mobile-friendly experience to answer basic questions (prices, location, hours) and confirm that this salon is a good fit.

Key success metrics (measurable):

Online bookings per month: How many appointment requests are made through the website’s booking system (track via booking tool analytics or Google Analytics goal completions)

.

Contact actions: Clicks on the phone number (tap-to-call) and WhatsApp chat button from the site (tracked as events), indicating visitors initiating contact.

Conversion rate: Percentage of site visitors who take a desired action (e.g. book, call, or message) – e.g. X bookings per Y unique visitors. This shows how effectively the site turns traffic into clients

.

New client inquiries: Number of first-time clients citing the website (or coming via online booking) each month. This can be measured by asking “How did you hear about us?” or tracking referral links.

Engagement & retention: Social media follow-through (clicks to Instagram profile) and repeat bookings. For example, track if visitors click through to IG or leave a review. Also monitor client retention rate (how many rebook) to see if the site’s info and ease of use encourages loyalty

.

2) Target users and intent

Women segment: Typically seeking hair services like cuts (e.g. trims, layers), coloring (highlights, balayage), styling for events, or treatments. They often value a portfolio of work – seeing photos of other women’s hair results to judge quality
salontoday.com
. Trust factors include the stylist’s experience with women’s hairstyles, use of quality products (to protect hair health), and a comfortable, safe environment. Women visitors will look for signals of professionalism: certifications or training (e.g. colorist courses), testimonials from other female clients, and clean, inviting salon setup photos. They may have concerns about a home-based salon’s professionalism or capabilities compared to a high-street salon, so the site must reassure them with before/after images, positive reviews, and clear service information. Showing that the stylist stays current on trends (e.g. balayage techniques) and has a personal touch (one-on-one attention in a private setting) will appeal to this group. Pricing transparency is crucial too – many women will look for a price list to ensure the salon offers value and won’t spring surprises
htmlburger.com
salontoday.com
. Common objections to address for women: “Is he experienced with women’s hair?” (answered by showcasing female hairstyles he’s done and any relevant credentials), “Will I get a salon-quality result in a home setting?” (answered by reviews, photos, and explaining the professional-grade products and tools used), and “Is it safe and comfortable?” (answered by highlighting the one-client-at-a-time focus, hygiene measures, and perhaps a female testimonial speaking to the comfort level).

Men segment: Typically looking for haircuts (classic cuts, fades, etc.), beard trims, or shaves. Men often prioritize convenience and consistency – they want to know they can get a great cut without hassle. A key motivator is seeing that the stylist is skilled in men’s grooming; displaying photos of modern men’s cuts and beard work builds confidence. Men also value availability and speed: a survey showed that 33% of clients (70% of them men) consider a stylist’s availability their top factor when choosing, underscoring the need to show flexible hours and an easy booking process
salontoday.com
. Trust signals for men include the stylist’s qualifications (e.g. a Master Barber certificate or years of experience cutting men’s hair), a gallery of male clients (so they can see styles similar to what they want), and perhaps any male-oriented touches (like a classic barber chair or mention of precision in fades). Many men prefer a straightforward, no-frills experience, so the men’s section should have a clean design and direct calls-to-action (“Book a haircut”) without an overload of text. Social proof like reviews from male clients (“Best barber I’ve found in Amsterdam...”) will resonate. Men might also be curious about practical details: parking availability, how long a typical appointment takes, or if walk-ins are possible (for a home salon, likely by appointment only – which should be clearly stated). Objections to address: “Is this a legit barbershop quality?” (answered by emphasizing professional equipment, sanitation – e.g. clean tools, fresh blades – and showcasing crisp haircut photos), “Do I need to bring anything or prepare?” (answered via a FAQ or note that everything is provided), and “Will it be awkward going to someone’s home?” (answered by showing a dedicated salon area and noting it’s a registered business with a welcoming atmosphere).

Tailoring content to each segment: The site’s split design means women and men will each see content crafted for their priorities. The women’s path will use a slightly softer tone and visuals: e.g. images of women with stylish cuts and color, maybe mention of care (conditioning treatments, etc.), and elegant design touches. The men’s path will have a more minimalist, bold vibe (possibly darker theme), with images of men’s cuts/beards, and straight-to-the-point text about services. Both segments will be given reasons to trust the salon’s quality and friendliness – just framed in ways that speak to their interests.

Key objections & how the site answers them:

“Can I trust the quality?” – Answered by prominent before-and-after photos and an Instagram feed of real client results, plus a “Reviews” section with genuine testimonials from happy customers (ideally gender-matched: women clients praising color results, men praising consistent cuts)
htmlburger.com
. Also mention any awards or certifications (e.g. trained under a known academy, certified color specialist) to boost credibility.

“Is a home salon professional and safe?” – Addressed by an “About the Salon” blurb explaining the setup (e.g. a dedicated room equipped like a pro salon), hygiene practices (clean tools, one client at a time means personal attention and sanitation), and business legitimacy (licensed stylist, insured, etc.). Including a photo of the salon interior can alleviate concerns by showing a clean, salon-like environment even if it’s at home.

“Do they handle [specific service] well?” – Addressed by clearly listing services for each audience with detail. For women: describe expertise in coloring, long-layered cuts, bridal styling if offered; for men: mention skills in skin fades, scissor cuts, beard sculpting, etc. If some services overlap (e.g. both might want hair treatments), list them in each context or in a unisex section. By reading the dedicated service info, the user should feel “this salon specializes in exactly what I need.” Any niche offerings (e.g. “hot towel shave” or “ombre coloring”) can be highlighted to show range.

“Is it easy to get an appointment?” – Answered by making the online booking system highly visible and simple, and stating things like “open evenings/weekends” or “flexible scheduling” if true. Emphasize the convenience: for example, mention that clients can self-book online 24/7, which many prefer
salontoday.com
. Also, provide contact options (WhatsApp, phone) for those who have questions – this assures visitors that it’s easy to communicate and schedule.

“What if I need to cancel or I’m unhappy?” – Answered by a short note on the policies: e.g. “24-hour cancellation policy, no charge if you reschedule timely” and possibly a satisfaction guarantee (“If you need a minor adjustment to your cut, let us know within 7 days”). This kind of transparency in a FAQ or policy section builds trust that the business is customer-service oriented.

By addressing these concerns in the content, the site will connect with each target segment on their own terms. This two-path approach should make women feel understood and valued as salon clients, and men feel comfortable that this is a barber-quality service, all under the umbrella of the same brand.

Recommendation: For this project, Option A (WordPress) is the recommended platform. It offers the right mix of customization and ease-of-use for a local salon. 5) Core user flows (step-by-step)

Flow 1: New Visitor – Browse and Book
Scenario: A first-time visitor (say, Jane) hears about the salon via a friend or finds it on Google. She wants to explore the website and possibly book an appointment.

Step 1: Landing on Entry Page – Jane types in the website URL (or clicks from Google search) and lands on the split entry page. She immediately sees two options: “Women” and “Men.” Since she’s interested in women’s hair services, she clicks “Women.” (If she had landed on a specific subpage via search – e.g. Google might sometimes send someone directly to “Women’s Services” – the site should still allow her to navigate between sections, but here we assume the front door.)

Step 2: Enter Women’s Mini-site – After clicking, Jane is taken to the Women Home page. Here, a hero image of a stylish woman’s haircut and a tagline (“Get the hair you love – personalized just for you”) greet her. A prominent “Book Appointment” button is already visible in the hero section, but she decides to scroll and explore first.

Step 3: Browse Services – Jane scrolls or navigates to Women’s Services (maybe the home page has a short teaser and a “View All Services” link). On the Services page, she sees a list of offerings with descriptions: haircuts, coloring, etc., each with prices or price ranges. She finds this reassuring – the site is upfront about what’s offered and the cost (e.g. Women’s cut €50, Highlights from €80)
htmlburger.com
. She notices mention of a complementary consultation included, which adds value. This answers her questions about if the service she wants is available and what it might cost.

Step 4: Check Portfolio – Next, she clicks the Gallery page to see actual results. A grid of before-and-after hair transformations and Instagram photos appears. She taps one or two to view larger – the images are high-quality and show diverse styles (blonde highlights, bridal updo, etc.). Impressed by the work, she’s now feeling confident in the stylist’s ability. (The gallery might actually be implemented as an Instagram feed slider on the home page, which she could scroll through without leaving the page – but the flow is similar: she’s consuming visual proof.)

Step 5: Read a Testimonial – Perhaps on the same page or a separate Reviews page, she sees a testimonial: “★ ★ ★ ★ ★ I got the best balayage from him – my friends all love it!” This social proof (especially from another female client) helps overcome any remaining doubt. Jane also clicks the About page to quickly read about the stylist – she sees he has 10+ years experience and trained in a top academy, which is a relief to know. The about page also mentions the cozy one-client setup, turning what could be a concern (home-based) into a plus (private and attentive).

Step 6: Initiate Booking – Now convinced, Jane is ready to book. She clicks the “Book Now” button (either in the site’s header or a CTA section – likely present consistently on all pages or as a sticky button). This takes her to the Booking page or triggers the scheduling widget overlay. On the Booking interface, she selects her desired service (e.g. “Full Highlights”) from a dropdown. The interface then shows a calendar – she picks a date and time slot from the available openings. She enters her name, phone, and email when prompted. She also sees a note about the salon address and an option to add a note (she writes “Looking for a subtle sun-kissed look”).

Step 7: Confirmation – She hits “Confirm Appointment.” Immediately, she sees a confirmation message on the site (“Thank you, your appointment is booked! An email confirmation has been sent.”). She also receives an email (or SMS) confirmation via the booking system. The site might present her with helpful info post-booking: e.g. “See you on [Date]! Here’s our location [link to map].”

Step 8: Follow-up/Engagement – After booking, Jane might browse a bit more or close the site. The site can gently encourage further engagement: perhaps a message like “Follow us on Instagram to see our latest styles” with the IG icon, which she clicks out of curiosity. On the Instagram profile, she might follow the salon. This keeps the relationship warm before her appointment.

Alternate path: If at any point Jane wasn’t ready to book online, the site also provided a phone number and WhatsApp link. She could have easily clicked the WhatsApp button (especially if on mobile) to message the salon for a question. The goal was to give her a frictionless path to conversion – in this flow, she booked directly online (preferred), but the presence of multiple contact options ensures whichever way she felt most comfortable is available.

Flow 2: Returning Visitor – Quick Rebooking in 30 seconds
Scenario: A returning client (say, Mark) has been to the salon before and just wants to book another appointment as fast as possible. He’s already familiar with the quality and just needs to snag a slot.

Step 1: Landing – Mark either types the URL or, more likely, has the site bookmarked. It might even be bookmarked to the Men’s section or booking page. If not, he lands on the entry page. Because he’s been here, he knows to click “Men” right away. (If we implemented a “remember selection” cookie, possibly he would be auto-routed to the Men section homepage to save a click.)

Step 2: Straight to Booking – On the Men’s homepage, Mark sees the familiar layout with the “Book Appointment” button right at the top. He doesn’t need to scroll or read anything. He clicks Book Now immediately. (If on mobile, the site’s design ensures the CTA is very visible without hunting – possibly a sticky “Book” button in the nav bar or a floating icon on small screens to always provide one-tap booking access.)

Step 3: Select Service/Time – The booking widget appears (or booking page loads). Mark chooses “Men’s Haircut” (perhaps it’s pre-selected if the system remembers his last service via login or cookie). The calendar shows upcoming time slots. He picks an available time next week at 6pm – this takes just a couple of taps. Since he’s a returning user, if the system has his details (some booking systems allow user accounts or recognizing by phone number/email), the form might be pre-filled. If not, he quickly enters name/phone (30 seconds is plenty if fields are minimal).

Step 4: Confirm – He hits confirm and instantly gets the confirmation message. Done – in half a minute he went from homepage to booked. This is the ideal optimized flow for repeat clients.

Step 5: (Optional) Bypass entry – To make this even faster, we might give Mark a way to skip the entry page entirely: for example, if we provide him (and all clients) with a direct link to the Men’s booking page in their confirmation email or via a QR code on a business card. But even without that, the site’s simplicity (one click at entry, one click to book, fill form, submit) makes it very quick. Research indicates that 42% of people prefer to self-book online rather than call
salontoday.com
, so ensuring this flow is smooth will keep those clients happy.

Alternate outcome: If Mark had an issue booking (say he can’t find a slot), he might then use the site’s quick contact – e.g. clicking the phone number to call and ask if any accommodation can be made. The site provides that backup. Also, since Mark is rebooking, he may not even visit the homepage – he could have the booking page saved or use a browser auto-complete that drops him into the booking form directly. The site should handle that gracefully (i.e. the booking page loads fine as a standalone). We’ll implement booking as a standalone page or clearly accessible route so returning users can indeed bookmark it.

Flow 3: Instagram Visitor – Trust & Contact
Scenario: A potential client (Emma) discovers the salon through Instagram. She saw a promoted post or a friend tagged the salon. She clicks the link in the Instagram bio (which typically is the website URL). This kind of visitor already likes the visual style (from IG) but needs more info and assurance before reaching out.

Step 1: Landing via Instagram – Emma clicks the bio link and lands on the website – likely on the entry page (unless we set the bio link to a specific section; some businesses might use a Linktree or have separate landing page, but here assume main URL). She’s not sure if the salon is mostly for men or women. On the split screen, she’ll likely choose based on her interest – suppose Emma is looking for a color treatment for herself, so she taps “Women.”

Step 2: Quick Impression on Women Home – Immediately on the Women’s homepage, she sees branding and vibe consistent with the Instagram she came from: same turquoise/teal accents, similar images. This consistency is good for trust (she knows she’s in the right place). The tagline “Home-based salon – Professional results for women” catches her eye and addresses the home salon aspect. She scrolls just a bit and sees an embedded Instagram feed carousel on the page, showing the latest 5 posts (which might include the very post that brought her here). This repetition reinforces credibility (“Yes, it’s the same stylist whose work I liked on IG”).

Step 3: Seek Trust Signals – Because Instagram focuses on visuals, Emma already saw styles she likes. Now on the site, she looks for additional trust signals beyond IG: she clicks Reviews. She reads a couple of glowing reviews from other clients. This is important because while Instagram showed skill, reviews show reliability and satisfaction. She’s thinking, “The hair looks great and people also say good things – nice.”

Step 4: Learn Details – Next, she checks a Service or Pricing page briefly to see if what she wants (e.g. balayage) is offered and to get an idea of price. She finds it listed, perhaps with a note “Consultation required for exact quote.” At least she knows it’s available.

Step 5: Contact via WhatsApp – Instagram users often prefer quick, informal contact. The site has a WhatsApp chat button fixed at the bottom or on Contact page (“Chat via WhatsApp”). Emma clicks that. It either opens WhatsApp Web or the app on her phone with a pre-filled message like “Hi, I’d like to ask about a hair service…”. She sends a message asking a couple of questions (availability or a specific color request). Because the owner manages WhatsApp, he responds quickly. This conversation (facilitated by the site’s easy chat link) converts Emma from a curious IG follower to an engaged lead.

Step 6: Booking – After a few questions, Emma is ready to book. The owner can even book her manually at this point, or he might direct her to the online booking link for formality. If she needs to use the site to finalize, she will click the Book Now button and go through the scheduling steps as in Flow 1. But the key in this flow is that the site provided enough trust and information to supplement Instagram: IG had the portfolio, but the site added prices, reviews, and a direct communication channel.

Alternate path: Some Instagram visitors might click the bio link and land directly on a special landing page (sometimes businesses use the link to go to a page with specific promos or combined content). If we wanted, we could create a “Instagram Landing” that is basically a simple page with quick links: e.g. “Book an appointment”, “See services”, etc., to cater to social traffic. However, since our homepage is simple, it might double as that already. The main point is that the website should carry forward the momentum from social media – this means showing the same style of work and then capturing the lead. If the IG visitor wasn’t ready to contact, they might at least follow a call-to-action like “Subscribe to our newsletter for tips” or just take note of the info and come back later. But our success measure here is likely that they either book or reach out (WhatsApp, etc.) after visiting, which the above flow achieves.

In all flows, we notice some common design implications:

The Women/Men choice adds one click for the user, but it significantly tailors their subsequent journey. To mitigate any friction, the choice page is ultra-simple and fast. Users in all examples had no confusion choosing their segment (the labeling and imagery will make it obvious).

We keep persistent CTAs (like a top “Book” button or a floating chat icon) so that no matter what page the user is on, conversion is one tap away. This is critical for Flow 2 and generally good for all flows – if a user decides “I’m convinced, let’s book,” they shouldn’t have to hunt for it.

The flows illustrate the importance of speed: returning users got to booking quickly, new users had clear paths to build trust then act. The site’s navigation and content structure supports these by being logical and uncluttered.

Finally, post-appointment retention could be considered part of flows: e.g., after Mark’s cut, perhaps the site (or integrated system) emails him a thank-you with a rebooking link or asks for a review (which feeds back into the content). While not on-site navigation, it’s part of the user experience cycle and something we can implement via the booking tool or an email list.

6) Design system: COLORS ONLY (with tokens)

We will establish two complementary color palettes: one for the Women’s theme and one for the Men’s theme, unified by a signature turquoise/teal brand color. Additionally, a set of neutral colors will be used across both for text and backgrounds. All colors are given in HEX codes, and each is assigned a role (CSS custom property token) for consistent use. The palettes are designed to meet accessibility (WCAG AA) contrast standards wherever applicable.

 

Women Theme Palette: A light, clean palette with a vibrant teal accent and a soft complementary tone. This evokes a modern, chic feeling.

--women-bg: #FFFFFF – Background for pages (women section). Pure white provides a clean canvas that makes images and text pop.

--women-surface: #F8F8F8 – Surface color for cards/sections on white (a very light cool gray). This can be used for section backgrounds (like a subtle block behind testimonials) to delineate areas while remaining airy.

--women-primary: #008080 – Primary CTA / Accent (Turquoise Teal). This is the brand’s core color, used for buttons, links, and highlights. It’s a medium-dark teal (mix of blue-green) that aligns with the owner’s desired turquoise/teal vibe. This color on white passes AA contrast for normal text (contrast ~4.77:1)
webcomm.nmsu.edu
, ensuring legibility. It will be featured in the logo and key buttons like “Book Now.”

--women-secondary: #FFBBB1 – Secondary Accent (Soft Coral Pink). A gentle pastel coral that complements teal. This might be used sparingly (e.g. hover states, decorative elements or to highlight prices). It adds a feminine, warm touch without overpowering. On the light theme it provides contrast from the teal (which is cool); however, it’s used carefully since coral on white has less contrast (mostly for small embellishments or icons, not text).

--women-text: #222222 – Primary text (Almost black). Used for body text on white backgrounds (e.g. service descriptions, paragraph text). Chosen for excellent contrast (nearly 21:1 on white) and a softer look than absolute black.

--women-text-muted: #6c757d – Muted text (Gray). For secondary information (e.g. captions, placeholders). This gray (~43% brightness) on white has ~4.7:1 contrast
webcomm.nmsu.edu
, which meets AA for normal text, so even muted text remains readable.

--women-border: #DDDDDD – Border color. A light gray for subtle borders or lines (e.g. under nav, around cards). It’s low-key to fit the clean theme.

Men Theme Palette: A bolder, darker palette that feels masculine and sleek, while still incorporating the signature teal.

--men-bg: #111111 – Background for pages (men section). A near-black deep charcoal. This gives the men’s pages a distinct, modern barbershop vibe (moody and stylish). It provides an excellent backdrop for light text and teal accents, creating contrast and drama.

--men-surface: #1E1E1E – Surface color for cards/sections on dark background. A slightly lighter charcoal (~12% gray). Use this for content sections or card backgrounds so they stand out just a bit from the page background (for example, a testimonial slider card or booking form might sit on this).

--men-primary: #009B8E – Primary CTA / Accent. A brighter teal variant (slightly more saturated and lighter than the women’s #008080) to pop on the dark background. On the nearly black background, this teal has a contrast ~4.4:1 against black – just shy of 4.5:1, but we will mostly use it for larger elements or alongside white text, not as small text on black. When used for buttons, we will put white text on it (white on this teal is ~4.0:1, so for normal text it’s a bit low; thus we may choose a slightly darker teal shade or semi-bold text to meet AA). Alternatively, we can outline buttons in teal with white text to increase contrast via font weight. Overall, this teal ties into the brand but adds energy on dark.

--men-secondary: #4E4E4E – Secondary Accent (Gray). On a dark theme, we can use a mid-gray for secondary buttons or elements (like an outline button or icon background). This mid-gray contrasts with the black background enough (and with white text on it). We avoid using something like coral here to maintain a more neutral, masculine aesthetic; instead, the teal does the accent job.

--men-text: #EEEEEE – Primary text (Very light gray). All main text on dark backgrounds will be near-white. Pure white (#FFFFFF) could be used, but a very light gray (#eee) is easier on the eyes against black, avoiding stark glare. Contrast vs #111 background is extremely high (~15:1), excellent for readability. Headings might use full white for extra punch, but body text can be #eee.

--men-text-muted: #AAAAAA – Muted text. A medium-light gray for secondary text on dark (e.g. subtitles, less important info). #aaa on #111 has ~6.9:1 contrast, which exceeds AA for normal text. It will appear clearly but distinct from primary text.

--men-border: #333333 – Border color. A dark gray for subtle dividers or outlines on the black background (e.g. dividing lines between list items). It’s visible but not distracting.

--neutral-900: #000000 – Black (used in typography in some cases, or overlays). We generally use the slightly lighter #111 for men-bg, but true black can appear in assets or needed contrast.

Color Usage Guidelines:

Consistency is key: Use the defined tokens rather than arbitrary colors to ensure the site looks unified. For instance, all primary buttons on both Women and Men sections use the respective --primary teal, so that the brand color is always present (this teal is the thread that connects the two themes).

Background vs Text: Always pair text colors with suitable backgrounds for readability. For example, use --women-text on --women-bg (dark charcoal on white) for standard paragraphs, which comfortably exceeds the 4.5:1 contrast ratio requirement
webcomm.nmsu.edu
. Conversely, on a --men-bg (dark background), use --men-text (light gray) for body text and perhaps pure white for large headings to maximize clarity. Avoid using medium or brand colors for long text on similar-toned backgrounds (e.g. do not put teal text on black or teal text on white for body copy, as those can be borderline). Instead, reserve brand colors for accent or UI elements and keep high contrast neutrals for large text blocks.

Primary vs Secondary CTA: The primary action buttons (like “Book Now”) should use the --primary teal with text in a color that meets contrast (likely white). Make sure the button text size is sufficiently large; white on our chosen teal (~4.0:1 for small text) is slightly under the strict AA threshold, so we will use at least semi-bold and a bit larger font on those buttons, or darken the teal by ~5-10% if needed to hit 4.5:1 for normal text size【11†】. Secondary buttons (like “Learn More” or less critical actions) can use either an outline style (teal border, transparent background with teal text for women’s theme – which on white background is fine contrast) or the --secondary color if in women’s section for a subtle look. On men’s dark theme, a secondary button might be a gray (--men-secondary) with white text. The idea is primary stands out most, secondary is more subdued.

Link and hover states: Text links in paragraphs will likely be styled with the primary teal (on light background, #008080 for women, which is distinguishable from black text and also an accustomed link color; on dark background, we might use the same teal or a slightly lighter shade for links against black). Ensure underline or a hover effect for clarity. On hover/focus, we can intensify or lighten the teal slightly (e.g. women’s teal hover to #00a0a0, men’s teal hover to #00B3A5) to indicate interactivity. These tiny variations should still maintain contrast (we’ll test that they don’t go too low).

Contrast and accessibility: All critical text color combinations are chosen to meet WCAG AA. For instance, our darkest text on light background and vice versa are well above 7:1 (AAA level). The brand teal against white is used for headings or large text mostly, or for shorter UI text where we can manage sizing. We avoid using the light coral on white for any important small text (its contrast is low, so it will only be decorative or perhaps in large header text where contrast can be lower if font is huge). Similarly, on the dark theme, we avoid mid-gray text on dark gray background for paragraphs – we stick to very light text for body. We also avoid color combinations that could be problematic for color-blind users – using a single accent (teal) reduces reliance on distinguishing multiple hues. When we do use the secondary coral (which is a red family) and teal (green family) together, we will not use them for adjacent status indicators that rely on color alone. They’re mostly separate contexts (coral more in women’s decorative context, teal as functional color).

Men & Women themes coherence: Both palettes share the core teal, albeit adjusted for context, so the site still feels like one brand. Neutrals are used to ensure content readability (white background for one, dark for other). If the owner’s preference is strongly toward turquoise, we might incorporate that in visuals as well (e.g. a teal filter overlay on hero images). But we will not overload every element with teal – it’s best used where we want user attention (CTAs, active menu item, etc.).

CTA contrast requirements: We ensure that the CTA button text is very readable against its background. For example, on a primary teal button, we use white text with sufficient size/boldness, achieving effective contrast
webcomm.nmsu.edu
. On a secondary outline button on the women theme (white button with teal border), we use teal text (contrast ~4.8:1 on white which is okay for normal text) but if the font is small, we might slightly darken the teal to ensure we don’t dip below 4.5:1. On the men theme’s dark background, a common pattern will be a teal button with white text (we may darken teal slightly to improve that contrast or simply make the text semi-bold and 1-2px larger, which qualifies as “large text” thus needing only 3:1 contrast
webaim.org
). In testing, if needed we’ll pick #007D7D for the teal on men’s to get a bit more contrast with white – but let’s assume our design uses at least 18px text on those buttons, making 4.0:1 acceptable (because 18px+ or 14px bold counts as large text under WCAG
webaim.org
). For safety, adjustments will be made to any combination that doesn’t meet AA for its text size. Finally, ensure any text over images has a text shadow or overlay if needed to maintain contrast (e.g. hero text over a photo might need a translucent dark overlay if the photo is light).

In summary, the color system revolves around the teal/turquoise brand color the owner chose, applied in a gender-sensitive way: lighter interface for Women, darker for Men. By following these tokens and guidelines, the site will have a coherent identity and remain accessible to all users.
